# Pitch IQ — Product Context

**Register:** product (an app/tool; the design serves the work, it is not the work)

## Product purpose
An agentic companion for a live football tournament (FIFA World Cup 2026). It does four
things, always grounded in real data: answers live match questions, pressure-tests
predictions against the market, writes pre-match briefings, and runs a bracket you can
lock and score in private friend leagues. The differentiator is the *agent*: it routes
intent, calls live-data tools, and reasons — and the interface should make that visible,
the way a good analyst shows their working.

## Users
Football fans on a second screen during knockout nights. They already know the game; they
want depth and speed, not a scoreboard. Two modes: (1) watching a match, asking "why six
minutes added", "what does this do to my bracket"; (2) between matches, setting picks and
checking the league. They are impatient with hype and with clutter.

## Brand & tone
Broadcast-grade and calm. The voice of a sharp analyst, not a hype man: precise, confident,
never breathless. Numbers are first-class. Nothing is fabricated; when data is missing we
say so. Sentence case, plain verbs, no exclamation marks, no emoji.

## Anti-references (do not become these)
- The generic dark SaaS dashboard with a single acid-green accent. (This is what the first
  build looked like; it reads "AI made that".)
- Betting-site neon, gradients-on-black, gamble-y urgency.
- Emoji-as-icon consumer apps.
- ESPN/broadcast clutter: ten widgets fighting for attention.

## Strategic principles
1. **Grounded over generated.** Every claim traces to a tool call. The UI surfaces that.
2. **The agent is visible.** Routing, the active specialist, tool calls, and streaming are
   shown as a legible run, not hidden behind a spinner.
3. **One moment of energy.** The interface is quiet ink; a single confident accent and a
   reserved live-red carry all the heat. Live state is the only thing that pulses.
4. **Data is typography.** Scores, clocks, probabilities use tabular figures and a
   condensed display face — the language of a broadcast lower-third.
